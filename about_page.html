Inspiration

What can technologies do and how they can affect us? The fictional relationship between the character and the hypothetic knowledge glasses tells a vivid story of the ethical challenges we might face when dealing with a piece of noble technology.

Our story is in the form of design fiction, which “incorporates speculative fiction and prototyping, juxtaposing materials and narrative to visualize and start conversations about a particular scenario (Bleecker, 2009). We use it not to imagine a future technology necessarily but, drawing on Langdon Winner (1986), to imagine forms of life that might materialize.”

Related Course Material

1. Utilitarianism and Duty
O'Neill, Onora "A Simplified Account of Kant's Ethics," in Gendler-Siegel-Cahn, The Elements of Philosophy (2008), New York: Oxford University Press, pp. 112-114
Kant’s ethics highlights the rule of conduct that is unconditional or absolute for all agents, the validity or claim of which does not depend on any desire or end. Henry cheats to pass the test to earn a doctor’s degree, which violates the universal moral law. Kant’s ethics suggests that it’s immoral for Henry to use the knowledge glasses anymore, not even to save the child from the disease. However, from a utilitarian point of view, saving the child shows benevolence and produces the greatest good. Moreover, if Henry is the only person who has the all-knowing power to save the child, then this would likely be his moral duty to help. Utilitarians think the end justifies the means because it’s morally good for Henry to use the machine and save the child. The decisions based on Kant’s ethics or utilitarianism is one of the main ethical tensions in the storyline.

2. Ethics and Digital Labor
Flash Forward, “Boss Bot,” September 29, 2020: https://www.flashforwardpod.com/2020/09/29/boss-bot/
A similar comparison is drawn between the Boss Bot and the hypothetical knowledge glasses as both utilize an on-demand mechanism to access information. The Boss Bot uses algorithms to match people to jobs based on certain personality metrics, while the knowledge glasses grant people access to any knowledge to their wish to learn. Though both technologies provide benefits like guaranteeing a job or accessing any information, the underlying problem is that they oversimplify the procedures that are supposed to be more human-involved. In an actual learning process, one needs to practice and reason about the knowledge. These procedures are often not considered as robust and systematic as digital labor, but they are essential to creating values as human beings.
